QUESTION


What are key genes for which we'd expect to see differential expression in the chemokine signaling pathway in colorectal cancer?

ANSWER


Key genes expected to show differential expression in the chemokine signaling pathway in colorectal cancer include:

1. CXCL13/CXCR5: This axis is involved in the pathogenesis, development, and metastasis of colorectal cancer through mechanisms such as upregulating MMP13 and activating the PI3K/Akt pathway (Gao, SH. et al., 2021).

2. CXCL12/CXCR4: CXCR4 is overexpressed in colorectal carcinoma tissues and is associated with disease progression and reduced survival. CXCL12 enhances chemotaxis, adhesion, proliferation, and ERK1/2 phosphorylation in colorectal cancer cells (Richard, CL. et al., 2008). Additionally, CXCL12 expression is often reduced in colorectal cancer due to DNA hypermethylation, which facilitates tumor cell migration (Goïta, AA. et al., 2022).

3. CXCL8/CXCR1/2: CXCL8 is markedly upregulated in colorectal carcinoma and is associated with tumor cell proliferation, migration, angiogenesis, and chemoresistance. The CXCL8-CXCR1/2 axis is crucial for tumor progression and metastasis (Liu, Q. et al., 2016)(Ha, H. et al., 2017).

4. CXCL2: CXCL2 is significantly overexpressed in colorectal cancer and is involved in tumor initiation, development, and angiogenesis. Knockdown of CXCL2 reduces the expression of EMT markers and MMPs, thereby inhibiting tumor proliferation and migration (Liu, Y. et al., 2020).

5. CXCL9, CXCL10, and CXCL13: These chemokines are associated with high densities of Th1/cytotoxic memory T cells and favorable prognosis in colorectal cancer. They are involved in building an efficient immune contexture at the tumor site (Mlecnik, B. et al., 2011)(Kocher, F. et al., 2022).

6. CCL19/CCR7: CCL19 inhibits tumorigenesis, metastasis, and angiogenesis in colorectal cancer and is associated with a good prognosis. It blocks angiogenesis by inhibiting the Met/ERK/Elk-1/HIF-1α/VEGF-A pathway in a CCR7-dependent manner (Stefani, C. et al., 2021).

These genes play significant roles in the chemokine signaling pathway and are critical for understanding the molecular mechanisms underlying colorectal cancer progression and potential therapeutic targets.

Notable genes and their associated proteins in the chemokine signaling pathway include:

1. Chemokines and Chemokine Receptors:
- CXCL12 and its receptors CXCR4 and ACKR3 (García-Cuesta, EM. et al., 2019).
- CCL2 (MCP-1) and its receptor CCR2 (Yu, W. et al., 2022).
- CCL5 (RANTES), CCL20 (MIP-3α), CXCL8 (IL-8), CXCL16 (SR-PSOX), and CX3CL1 (fractalkine) (Yu, W. et al., 2022).
- CXCR1, CXCR2, CXCR3, CXCR4, CXCR5 (CXC receptors) and CCR1, CCR2, CCR3, CCR4, CCR5, CCR6, CCR7, CCR8, CCR9 (CC receptors) (Ahmadiankia, N. et al., 2016).

2. Signaling Pathways and Molecules:
- PI3K/AKT pathway: Genes such as PIK3R3 and AKT (Sharma, M. et al., 2013)(Rizeq, B. et al., 2020).
- MAPK pathway: Genes such as MAPK4, MAPK9, MAP3K9, and components like ERK1/2 and p38 (Sharma, M. et al., 2013)(Verma, AK. et al., 2018).
- JAK/STAT pathway: Genes encoding Janus kinases (JAKs) and signal transducers and activators of transcription (STATs) (Soriano, SF. et al., 2002)(Rizeq, B. et al., 2020).
- NF-κB pathway: Genes involved in the activation of NF-κB and its regulatory components (Verma, AK. et al., 2018)(Lu, C. et al., 2020).
- PLC pathway: Genes encoding phospholipase C (PLC) and related molecules like PIP2, DAG, and IP3 (Huang, X. et al., 2022).

3. Other Key Genes:
- ADAM10: A protease responsible for cleaving CXCL16, which is a proposed diagnostic and prognostic marker for various cancers (Mir, H. et al., 2019).

These genes and their associated pathways play crucial roles in mediating the effects of chemokines on cell migration, proliferation, survival, and other cellular functions, particularly in the context of inflammation, immune responses, and cancer progression.

The genes that show differential expression in colorectal cancer (CRC) include:

1. NAT10: Identified as a regulator of mRNA acetylation with multiple functions in cancer (Zhang, H. et al., 2023).
2. LGR5, CLDN9, MYC, MEX3A: Differentially expressed between normal and CRC tissues, as well as between high and low NAT10 expression groups (Zhang, H. et al., 2023).
3. ARHGEF7: Overexpressed in CRC and associated with metastasis and poor survival (Kotelevets, L. et al., 2020).
4. APC, KRAS, PIK3CA, GPT2, MSH2, MLH1, TP53, CTNNB1, AKT1, MMP2, NRAS, CD274, STK11, CTLA4, PARK7, SNCA: Found to have somatic mutations in CRC (Cao, Y. et al., 2023).
5. SFRP4, AURKA, BMP7, CENPH, PTGER4, ID4: Differentially expressed between adenomas and carcinomas (Carvalho, B. et al., 2012).
6. NAT2, XDH, GPX3, AOC3, AKR1C4, SPHK1, ADCY5: Metabolism-related genes significantly related to the overall survival of CRC patients (Miao, Y. et al., 2020).
7. JAG2: Increased expression in CRC cell lines compared to normal colorectal cell lines (He, W. et al., 2019).
8. NEUROG1, REG1B, CMIP, KPNA3: Identified as differentially expressed genes in CRC (Wang, XW. et al., 2023).
9. APC, BAX, BCL2, DCC, KRAS, MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, SMAD2, SMAD4, TGFBR2, TP53: Frequently involved in CRC and defined in KEGG colorectal cancer MSI and CIN signaling pathways (Wang, F. et al., 2014).

These genes have been identified through various studies and datasets, indicating their significant roles in the pathogenesis and progression of colorectal cancer.
